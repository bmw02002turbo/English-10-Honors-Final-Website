Longer Stories: Our Other Specialty


The Rising Sun

By Bryan Hsu

It was shaping up to be a clear, cloudless day, though Kawamura’s mind was anything but. Living on an idyllic Pacific island, it was easy to lose yourself in the azure water, golden beaches, and a glorious rising sun peeking over the horizon. So easy, in fact, that if you stood on said beach, you could almost ignore the gunship docked off shore, the complex behind you, or the shouts of the platoon jogging across the sand in time. Indeed, the calming water lapping onto the shore was almost enough to tear his mind away from the fact that Kawamura didn’t want to be here with every fiber of his being.

As a draftee, Kawamura was an unwilling participant. Combined with his Christian faith, he wanted nothing more than to tear up and forget about his draft card. But he and his family both knew that if he didn’t show up, they would receive a visit from the kempeitai* that would not end well for him and his family. So begrudgingly, he arrived at the muster station and boarded the train that would take him away to the training camp. Through freezing winter rains, Kawamura slogged through training, eventually being judged unfit for service and quietly sent to be a guard, the supposed regulatory arm of the military that was actually a dumping ground for the rejects.

That’s how he ended up on a remote Pacific atoll, guarding an empty complex. Far, far away, the remaining mass of the Navy was steaming away from him and the small contingent stationed on the island. The American airmen had left long ago, and there was nothing to do but squint at the horizon to hopefully catch a glimpse of the smoke of said fleet. He muttered aloud a thought he had kept within himself for quite a while.

“Why do we fight?”

“To win the war, of course.” The Lieutenant had finished leading his run and had made his way beside Kawamura. Without taking his eyes off the dawn, Kawamura replied, “That doesn’t make any sense. What’s in it for us? Why should we follow the orders of those in the ivory tower?” The Lieutenant, also looking off into the distance, drawled, “That sounds a lot like treason to me.”

Kawamura whipped around angrily. “You don’t get to tell me what is treason and what isn’t! I am a loyal citizen, I pay my taxes, I-” “I wasn’t disagreeing with you there,” the officer interrupted coolly. “You know those poor schmucks in the fleet? There’ll be hell to pay at Midway, I can feel it.** And you don’t think they can feel it too?”

The Lieutenant turned around to face him. “Mr. Kawamura, do you believe in God?”

“I do, sir.”

“And do you believe that this war is justified in His eyes? That we are fighting for the moral victory?”

“I do not, sir.”

“Then there you have it. Our war was fought out of an innately selfish desire, and it’s that selfishness that keeps us going. On a wider level, the annexation of the Philippines and eventually the rest of Southeast Asia for natural resources. Oil, rubber, ores, laborers, these are all things that the Empire needs. On a personal level, we fight to survive. We are selfish in our desire to keep ourselves alive, and if that means taking away someone else’s life to fulfill that goal, so be it.”

“That’s an awfully pessimistic outlook to take.”

The Lieutenant shrugged. “It’s the only reasonable one, whether you like it or not.”

“But don’t you want to go back to your families, your children at home? Wouldn’t you say that the fight is born out of a desire to protect them, not out of any personal vision of survival against the odds?”

The Lieutenant peered over his wire-rimmed glasses at Kawamura. “Mr. Kawamura, I have no children back at home, and no more family, either. Pray that the anti-air crews are faster to the punch when the time comes to defend your family then they were mine.” He turned away, and stepped across the sand, leaving nothing but a trail of bootprints behind.

The incoming tide swept the prints away, and yet Kawamura remained, gazing at the rising sun.

*The kempeitai, or secret police, would still end up visiting Kawamura’s family after his death for the battle of Okinawa. He would be withdrawn from Execution Island and pressed into front-line service after the IJA suffered heavy losses late in the war. Ironically enough, he didn’t die of bullets or bombs in one of the bloodiest battles of the war, but a long hidden heart murmur exacerbated by dehydration and stress. Stay hydrated, kids.

**Midway would prove to be the finishing blow for the major elements of the Japanese navy. The loss of 4 carriers and numerous accompanying ships would weaken the IJN to the extent that it would not prove to be a significant threat in the later years of the war.
